# PC-98 Aesthetic -- Design Reference

PC-98 is an aesthetic originating from the NEC PC-9800 series of personal computers, dominant in Japan from the early 1980s to the late 1990s. The visual style is most closely associated with visual novels and eroge of the era, characterized by high-resolution pixel art (640x400), severely limited color palettes (16-256 colors from a 4096-color bank), and meticulous dithering techniques that create the illusion of depth and gradient with minimal colors. Artists placed pixels individually, exploiting dithering patterns to simulate smooth shading, soft skin tones, and atmospheric lighting within extreme technical constraints. The result is a uniquely warm, intimate, and melancholic visual language distinct from both Western pixel art and later anime CG.

---

## Visual Characteristics

- **High-resolution pixel art** at 640x400 -- far more detailed than contemporary 8-bit Western systems, allowing fine facial features, fabric folds, and architectural detail
- **Heavy dithering** -- checkerboard, ordered, and custom dither patterns used extensively to simulate gradients, soft shadows, and color transitions with a limited palette
- **Limited color palette** -- typically 16 simultaneous colors on screen, selected from a bank of 4096; some later games pushed to 256 colors
- **Anime and manga art style** -- character designs reflect 1980s-1990s anime conventions: large expressive eyes, detailed hair with highlights, slender figures
- **Close-up character portraits** -- compositions focus on face and upper-body close-ups of characters, often occupying the right half or center of the screen
- **Thick ornate decorative borders** -- game UI features elaborate frames around the main viewport, often with Art Nouveau-inspired flourishes, columns, floral motifs, or stained-glass patterns
- **Distinct UI panels** -- text boxes, status displays, date/time indicators, and menu areas are clearly delineated with decorative borders and distinct background fills
- **Stylized custom fonts** -- UI text often uses pixel-rendered Japanese characters (kanji, hiragana, katakana) with careful attention to legibility at low resolution
- **Soft, warm color temperature** -- despite limited palettes, artists favored warm pastels, soft pinks, lavenders, and muted earth tones
- **Visible pixel structure** -- individual pixels are discernible, but dithering creates an overall softness that distinguishes PC-98 art from the hard-edged blockiness of 8-bit systems
- **CRT display softening** -- original viewing on CRT monitors blended adjacent pixels slightly, making dithered areas appear as smooth gradients
- **Atmospheric backgrounds** -- detailed pixel-art environments (school buildings, city streets, interiors, nature scenes) rendered with careful perspective and dithered sky gradients
- **Emotional intimacy** -- compositions emphasize character emotion, eye contact, and personal moments rather than action or spectacle

## Color Palette

The PC-98 palette is defined by careful selection from a limited bank, with dithering used to extend perceived range:

- **16 simultaneous colors** typical per scene, chosen from a 4096-color (12-bit RGB) total bank
- **Warm pastels dominate** -- soft pinks, lavenders, cream, peach, and muted rose
- **Cool accents** -- slate blue, teal, muted purple, and dusty cyan for contrast and shadow
- **Skin tones** created through careful dithering of 2-4 colors (peach, pink, cream, light brown)
- **Hair rendered with highlight bands** -- 3-4 color steps from dark base to bright highlight, dithered at transitions
- **Backgrounds use earthy and atmospheric tones** -- olive green, dusty blue, warm gray, faded amber
- **Pure black used sparingly** for outlines, deep shadows, and text
- **Pure white used for specular highlights** on eyes, hair, and metallic surfaces
- **No smooth gradients** -- all transitions achieved through dithering patterns

## Typography

### Typeface Characteristics

PC-98 typography reflects the constraints of rendering complex Japanese characters on a 640x400 pixel grid:

- **Pixel-rendered characters** -- all text drawn on a fixed grid, typically 16x16 or 8x16 pixel cells for Japanese, 8x16 for Latin characters
- **Monospaced Japanese text** -- each kanji, hiragana, and katakana character occupies a uniform square cell
- **Half-width Latin characters** -- English text and numbers rendered at half the width of Japanese characters
- **Clean, legible bitmap forms** -- text prioritizes readability within the pixel grid over decorative flourish
- **White or cream text on dark backgrounds** -- high-contrast text for dialog boxes, typically on solid dark or black backgrounds
- **Decorative title screens** -- game titles rendered as elaborate pixel art logotypes with custom letterforms
- **UI labels in small pixel fonts** -- date, time, money, and status indicators use compact pixel fonts
- **Dialog text is the primary typographic element** -- visual novels are text-heavy; the dialog box is the most important UI component

## Key Design Elements and Motifs

### Ornate Decorative Borders

The defining UI element of PC-98 games -- elaborate frames surrounding the main viewport:

- **Art Nouveau-inspired columns and flourishes** along left and right edges
- **Floral and vine motifs** woven into border decorations (roses, leaves, scrollwork)
- **Stained-glass panel effects** with colored segments in border columns
- **Sculpted figure reliefs** (classical female figures, angels) integrated into border pillars
- **Layered border construction** -- inner frame, ornamental middle zone, outer frame
- **Metallic bronze/gold appearance** -- borders simulate carved or embossed metal

### Dialog Box

- **Solid dark background** (black or very dark navy) with high-contrast text
- **Positioned at bottom of screen**, spanning most of the width
- **Thin pixel border** (1-2px) in a contrasting warm color
- **Character name displayed** in a separate label area above or within the dialog box
- **Text appears character-by-character** in the original games (typewriter reveal)

### Status Panels

- **Date and time displays** in dedicated UI regions (often bottom-left)
- **Currency/stats counters** in bottom-right, using digital/segmented number fonts
- **Day-of-week indicators** with styled labels
- **Small pixel-art icons** for status indicators

### Dithering Patterns

The visual signature of the PC-98 aesthetic -- used everywhere to simulate gradients:

- **Checkerboard dithering** -- alternating pixels of two colors in a regular grid
- **Ordered dithering (Bayer matrix)** -- systematic dot patterns creating tonal steps
- **Horizontal-line dithering** -- alternating rows of two colors for subtle horizontal banding
- **Custom artistic dithering** -- hand-placed pixels in organic patterns for skin, hair, and fabric
- **Gradient simulation** -- progressive density changes in dither pattern to suggest smooth transitions

### Character Art

- **Large, detailed character portraits** -- the focal point of most screens
- **1980s-1990s anime style** -- large eyes, detailed hair with multi-color highlights, expressive faces
- **Skin rendered with warm dithered tones** -- peach, pink, cream blended through dithering
- **Hair with prominent highlight bands** -- bright streaks suggesting shine, achieved with 3-4 color steps
- **Clothing with fabric texture** -- dithered folds, creases, and patterns
- **Characters positioned in right half** or center of viewport, with background scene behind

### Background Environments

- **Detailed architectural scenes** -- schools, city streets, residential interiors, parks
- **Atmospheric sky gradients** -- sunset and twilight skies created through extensive dithering
- **Perspective-correct environments** -- careful vanishing-point construction despite pixel limitations
- **Nature elements** -- trees, water, clouds rendered with dithered color transitions
- **Indoor scenes** with furniture, windows, and ambient lighting effects

---

## Layout Principles

- **Fixed 640x400 composition** -- the canonical PC-98 resolution; modern implementations should reference this 8:5 aspect ratio
- **Three-zone vertical layout** -- ornate border frame (sides), main viewport (center), dialog/UI strip (bottom)
- **Character portrait on one side, scene on the other** -- asymmetric compositions with character art overlapping or adjacent to background
- **Dialog box anchored to bottom** -- spanning the full width minus borders, with solid dark background
- **Status information in corners** -- date/time bottom-left, stats/money bottom-right
- **Generous border regions** -- decorative frames consume 10-15% of screen width on each side
- **Single-screen compositions** -- each scene is a complete tableau; no scrolling in original games
- **Dark backgrounds for UI regions** -- pure black or near-black behind text for maximum readability
- **Content framed within viewport** -- all imagery contained within the ornate border frame

### Modern Web Adaptation

- Use the **ornate border as a page frame** -- decorative side panels flanking a central content area
- **Dialog box pattern** for content sections -- dark background panels with light text
- **Card-based layouts** with thick decorative borders echoing the PC-98 UI frames
- **Fixed or max-width containers** referencing the 8:5 aspect ratio
- **Dark mode by default** -- dark backgrounds with warm accent colors

## Imagery and Visual Content Guidelines

When creating or sourcing imagery for a PC-98 styled site:

- **Favor anime-style character illustrations** with visible pixel structure or retro styling
- **Use limited color palettes** -- restrict illustrations to 16-32 colors maximum for authenticity
- **Apply dithering to photographs** -- convert photos to limited-palette dithered versions using tools like Dither Me This or ImageMagick's ordered dither
- **Warm color temperature** -- all imagery should lean warm (pinks, peaches, golds) rather than cool
- **Close-up character compositions** -- faces and upper bodies, not wide action scenes
- **Soft, melancholic mood** -- twilight, sunset, quiet moments, rainy windows, school hallways
- **Interior and urban Japanese settings** -- classrooms, train stations, residential neighborhoods, parks
- **Avoid photorealism** -- everything should feel pixel-rendered or illustrated, not photographic
- **Scale pixel art at integer multiples** (2x, 3x, 4x) with `image-rendering: pixelated`
- **Frame imagery within decorative borders** -- images should feel contained in a viewport, not edge-to-edge

## Related Aesthetics

| Aesthetic | Relationship to PC-98 |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **8-Bit** | Earlier generation; PC-98 is far more detailed with higher resolution and more colors, but shares pixel art foundation |
| **Animecore** | Overlapping visual language; PC-98 is the pixel-art-era predecessor of digital anime aesthetics |
| **Cassette Futurism** | Shares retro-tech nostalgia and 1980s-90s Japanese computing culture |
| **Cyberpunk** | Some PC-98 games (Policenauts, Night Slave) share cyberpunk themes and neon-accented visuals |
| **Future Funk** | Borrows PC-98 and city pop era imagery for nostalgic Japanese retro-futurism |
| **Heisei Retro** | PC-98 is a core artifact of Heisei-era (1989-2019) Japanese media culture |
| **Lo-fi** | Shares melancholic, low-fidelity, nostalgic mood; PC-98 screenshots are common lo-fi visual elements |
| **Pixel UI** | Western parallel development of pixel-based interface design during the same era |
| **Retrofuturism** | PC-98 represents a Japanese branch of retro computing culture |
| **Vaporwave** | Heavily draws from PC-98 limited palettes and nostalgic Japanese computing imagery |

## Implementation Notes

- The PC-98 aesthetic is **warm and intimate**, not cold or clinical. Avoid the temptation to make it look like generic retro-tech. The defining mood is soft melancholy, not hard-edged futurism.
- **Dithering is the visual signature** -- without visible dithering patterns, the result will look like generic dark-mode anime, not PC-98. Use CSS checkerboard patterns, stepped gradients, and pixelated textures.
- **Ornate borders are essential** -- the elaborate decorative UI frames are what immediately distinguish PC-98 games from other pixel art styles. Even a simplified version with layered box-shadows and warm bronze borders evokes the aesthetic.
- **CRT viewing context matters** -- original PC-98 art was designed for CRT monitors that softened pixels and blended dithered colors. A subtle scanline overlay and slight blur can approximate this. Without it, dithering patterns appear harsher than intended.
- **Color restraint is critical** -- resist using more than 12-16 distinct colors in any single view. The beauty of PC-98 art comes from maximizing visual richness within severe limitations.
- **Sound design** complements the aesthetic: FM synthesis music (Yamaha YM2203/YM2608 chip sound) with bright, clear, bell-like tones. Consider ambient chiptune audio loops for immersive implementations.
- **Dialog-driven interaction** -- the visual novel format means text is the primary interactive element. Design for reading comfort with generous line-height, warm text colors, and dark dialog box backgrounds.
